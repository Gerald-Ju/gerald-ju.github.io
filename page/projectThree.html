Applying Machine Learning and Audio Analysis Techniques to Katydid Acoustic Signals

Published March 15, 2022

Image origin: Sebastian Oberst

Insects communicate in a variety of different ways in nature. They, like humans communicate through their various sensors like smell and taste that can detect chemical signals, sight through different markings and colours as well as acoustics. The sounds they create produce differing patterns for mating calls and warning calls.

Katydids, from the family Tettigoniidae, also known as the bush cricket or long-horned grasshopper (Kara Rogers, 2015), produce varying sounds for communication. Through sound signals, katydids expand their effect range of communication (Tiwari, 2020), compared to sight, smell and touch whose range is limited. They can become inconspicuous at a moments notice by simply pausing. And like the human language, each species have similar yet different call signals.

PyTorch, an open-source machine learning framework was used in the project due to ease of learning and providing abstractions to reduce boilerplate code, resulting in faster model development. I had cut and converted the sample audio signals of the katydids into mel spectrograms which then allowed me to run them through a convolutional neural network (CNN) based off of the TinyVGG CNN. The results of this project however, can not currently be shared.
